Statue of Liberty

Mother of Exiles

The Statue of Liberty is a colossal neoclassical sculpture on Liberty Island in New York Harbor. The copper statue, a gift from the people of France to the people of the United States, was designed by French sculptor Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi and built by Gustave Eiffel. The statue was dedicated on October 28, 1886.

Between 1886 and 1924, almost 14 million immigrants entered the United States through New York. The Statue of Liberty was a reassuring sign that they had arrived in the land of their dreams. To these anxious newcomers, the Statue’s uplifted torch did not suggest “enlightenment,” as her creators intended, but rather, “welcome.” Over time, Liberty emerged as the “Mother of Exiles,” a symbol of hope to generations of immigrants.

The opening of the immigrant processing station at Ellis Island in 1892 in the shadow of the Statue of Liberty facilitated an immigrant association, as did the later popularity of Emma Lazarus’s poem, “The New Colossus.” In 1883, Lazarus donated her poem to an auction raising funds for the construction of the Statue’s pedestal. In 1903 the poem was engraved on a bronze plaque and affixed to the base of the Statue.

The Statue was designated as a National Monument in 1924 by President Calvin Coolidge. In 1937 President Franklin D. Roosevelt expanded the monument to include all of Liberty Island (known than as Bedloe’s Island). Ellis Island was made part of the Statue of Liberty National Monument by President Lyndon Johnson in 1965, further cementing the Statue of Liberty’s association with immigration.
